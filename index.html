Phishing Attacks: Recognizing the Threat

What is Phishing?

Phishing is a fraudulent attempt to obtain sensitive information by disguising communications as trustworthy sources. Attackers impersonate legitimate organizations to trick users into revealing passwords, credit card numbers, or personal data.

Did You Know?

90% of data breaches start with phishing emails

Common Phishing Indicators

Suspicious Links

URLs that don't match the sender

Urgency Tactics

"Act now" or "Confirm immediately"

Generic Greetings

"Dear Customer" instead of your name

Poor Grammar

Spelling errors and awkward language

Real-World Examples

✓ LEGITIMATE

Why it's legitimate:

  • Official PayPal domain (paypal.com)
  • Personalized greeting with real name
  • Professional formatting
  • Suggests going to official website
✗ PHISHING

Red flags:

  • Domain typo (paypa1.com vs paypal.com)
  • Generic greeting
  • Spelling errors ("imediately")
  • Urgency and threats
  • Direct link instead of website

Best Practices to Avoid Phishing

1
Verify the Sender

Check the email address carefully - phishers often use similar domains

2
Hover Before Clicking

Hover over links to see the actual URL before clicking

3
Visit Official Sites Directly

Go to the official website instead of clicking email links

4
Report Suspicious Emails

Forward phishing attempts to your IT department

Password Security: Creating Strong Credentials

Password security is about using credentials that are hard to guess, unique for every account, and protected from reuse after a breach. Strong passwords or passphrases should be long, unpredictable, and stored in a trusted password manager rather than written down or saved in plain text.

What You Should Practice

  • Use a unique password for each website, app, and work system.
  • Create long passwords or passphrases that avoid names, birthdays, and common words.
  • Use a reputable password manager to generate and store credentials securely.
  • Enable multi-factor authentication so a stolen password alone cannot unlock the account.

Malware Protection: Identifying Threats

Malware is malicious software designed to steal data, spy on activity, damage systems, or block access until a ransom is paid. It commonly spreads through unsafe downloads, infected attachments, fake updates, malicious links, pirated software, and unknown USB devices.

How to Reduce Risk

  • Install software only from trusted official sources.
  • Keep your operating system, browser, and applications updated.
  • Do not open unexpected attachments or plug in unknown removable media.
  • Report unusual pop-ups, slow performance, disabled security tools, or unfamiliar programs.

Social Engineering: Recognizing Manipulation

Social engineering attacks target people instead of systems. Attackers use trust, urgency, authority, fear, curiosity, or helpfulness to pressure someone into revealing information, sending money, approving access, or bypassing normal security steps.

Common Warning Signs

  • A caller, email, or message asks for passwords, one-time codes, or confidential data.
  • The request creates pressure to act immediately or keep the conversation secret.
  • The sender claims to be an executive, IT support, bank employee, vendor, or government official without proof.
  • You are asked to use an unusual payment method, download a tool, or approve a login you did not start.

Data Protection: Securing Information

Data protection means keeping personal, financial, customer, and company information confidential, accurate, and available only to authorized people. Good handling practices reduce the chance of accidental exposure, identity theft, compliance violations, and business disruption.

Safe Data Handling

  • Share sensitive files only with approved people through approved systems.
  • Lock screens and secure paper documents when leaving your workspace.
  • Use encryption, access controls, and secure backup processes where available.
  • Dispose of old devices and documents through approved wiping, shredding, or destruction methods.

Network Security: Safe Online Practices

Network security protects devices and data while they communicate over WiFi, office networks, home routers, cloud services, and the internet. Safe habits are especially important on public networks, where attackers may intercept traffic, imitate trusted hotspots, or trick users into unsafe websites.

Everyday Network Safety

  • Verify public WiFi names with staff before connecting.
  • Use a VPN or mobile data for sensitive work on untrusted networks.
  • Check for HTTPS and avoid entering credentials on suspicious pages.
  • Keep home routers updated and protected with strong administrator and WiFi passwords.
